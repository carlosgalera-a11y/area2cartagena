# Thoracostomy — Finger Thoracostomy

- Tipo: procedure
- Categoría: Breathing
- Entorno: adult
- Indication ID: 2793


## Tab: Preparation
## Indications
    Tension pneumothorax Massive hemothorax Thoracic trauma with severe respiratory distress or hemodynamic compromise Failure or inefficiency of a needle thoracentesis (needle decompression)
## Contraindications
  ### Absolute
    None
## Anatomy
    
## Equipment Preparation
    Sterile gloves Antiseptic solution Scalpel Hemostatic forceps (optional but useful) Sterile compresses Occlusive dressing

## Tab: Execution
## 1
    Patient position Patient lying on their back If possible slightly inclined on the opposite side If possible arm abduction > 90 degrees
## 2
    
    Don personal protective equipment including visor Large disinfection of the skin (lateral thoracic region)
## 3
    
    Identify the 4th or 5th intercostal space at the anterior axillary line
## 4
    Make a horizontal incision of at least 4-5 cm through the full thickness of the skin  Cut skin and subcutaneous tissues until you feel the intercostal muscles
## 5
    
    
    With a gloved finger or hemostatic forceps, perform a clean and controlled dissection through the intercostal muscles to the pleura Pleural perforation
## 6
    
    Quickly explore with your finger to confirm the release and permeability of the pleural space Check for the absence of adhesions or blood clots obstructing the opening

## Tab: Aftercare
## Ensure the thoracostomy remains open
    Avoid patient's arm occluding thoracostomy site
    If recurrent tension physiology is suspected (patient deteriorates) Reinsert finger an confirm thoracostomy is open and lung is expanded
## Transition to chest tube
    As soon as possible, insert a chest tube with a drainage system
    [PROCEDURE] Thoracostomy — Tube Thoracostomy (Chest Tube)
## If chest tube insertion delayed
    Cover wound with rectangular sterile dressing that is securely taped on only 3 sides (as for open pneumothorax)
## Acute (< 24 hours) complications
    Poor positioning/failure (e.g. extrapleural, below diaphragm) Thoracostomy closure with recurrent tension physiology Laceration of internal thoracic structures (e.g. blood vessels, lung, heart if left sided) Open pneumothorax
